> Adapted from a Zhihu Q&A: [When you're in extreme pain and feel you can't go on, what do you do?](https://www.zhihu.com/question/432162235/answer/1971315409295746124), by @如是行者. Rights belong to the original author. Body text extracted from the offline page saved in your `raw/` folder.

Feeling you **cannot go on** often has two layers, the author argues: not accepting present reality (pain), and not seeing a way to change it (loss of hope).

More deeply: you resist what happens, expecting life to follow your script—and when it does not, pain escalates to the limit.

Below are three themes from the answer.

## 1. Pain is only a feeling (Wang Yangming)

The author cites Wang Yangming: beyond human affairs and change, there is nothing else; joy, anger, grief, and fear are all part of human experience. What we call success or failure with money, status, love, or loss is, at bottom, **how we relate to our own sensations, emotions, and thoughts**.

**Success** is often felt as pride, safety, release, fulfillment.  
**Failure** as insecurity, fear, disgust, guilt.

Fear of failure, loss, or ridicule is really dislike of the **bodily discomfort** they trigger. Pain is a physical feeling—you want relief, escape.

The advice: **do not flee**. Sit with sorrow and pain. Thoughts, moods, and aches come and go; they are not fully under your control.

If they are not truly "yours," why run? You label them "pain," but they are sensations—prolonged because you react and fight them.

## 2. Nothing lasts forever (impermanence)

The author quotes Dilgo Khyentse Rinpoche: with age, one learns **confidence in life's unfolding**—whatever happens may point to a larger, fuller plan beyond what we can see in the moment.

We judge life in the short term: good luck vs blow. Over time, much that hurt becomes growth; much that seemed hopeless resolves oddly.

Wisdom is not getting every wish, but **trusting what occurs**—not blind optimism, but seeing that reality is wider than our plans. Sometimes life arranges better than we do.

Whatever the present case, keep walking with trust; gain and loss, ease and hardship, may all fit a direction we cannot see yet.

## 3. Facts are maximal: allow everything

Truly wise and strong people, the author says, do not war with what "should not" happen—they **allow**, then respond and act as conditions allow.

What happens is cause and condition: when conditions gather, it arises; when they end, it fades. Blocking that trend starts pain—you insist on another possibility, yet all possibilities were always open; that is impermanence. Complaint only harms you.

Examples: "Why is life so hard?" "Why me?" "How can I have this thought or mood?"

When you say events, thoughts, or feelings **should not** be, you hurt yourself. What we can do is **allow**—facts do not bend; thoughts and feelings have no fixed moral weight; let them come and watch them go. Resistance amplifies them.

Whether you fear what happens or not, it still happens. So why not accept?

Life is experience. Full acceptance can bring calm, stability, and strength.
