## 1. What does “objective” mean?

To ask whether indeterminacy is “objectively real,” you must define **objective**—usually **independent of observer/description**.

But talking about “objective” often already assumes you can describe a system **without** the observer. The author argues that clashes with a Heisenberg-style reading. Heisenberg wrote:

> *What we observe is not nature itself but nature exposed to our method of questioning.*

We do not see “nature itself,” only what nature shows through **how we ask**. In that frame, “objective indeterminacy” in the usual sense is **not a meaningful question**.

For Heisenberg, physics describes **observables only**; attributing a “state” to an **unobserved** system is empty. Paths “come into being” through observation—we discuss outcomes, not hidden trajectories. In short: **measurement = reality**. With many identical systems, repeated measurements yield a distribution; only then can we speak of uncertainty (e.g., standard deviation).

From this starting point, there is little point splitting “uncertain” vs “cannot measure precisely”: **only measurable quantities qualify as state**; state uncertainty matches outcome uncertainty—**the two are the same**.

“Only measurement matters” can extend to “measurement **defines** state.” Unless “objective” is redefined, “objectively thus-and-so” has no role here.

## 2. How Heisenberg’s view evolved

Interpretations of the uncertainty relation varied; Heisenberg himself revised his story more than once.

His original German **anschaulich** is hard to translate (“visualizable” is closest)—the principle concerns **observables**, while their link to “state” depends on interpretation. Stripping interpretations, the shared point is: **a measurement outcome carries uncertainty**.

An early line was: observation **disturbs** the system, so results are uncertain. Position and momentum only make sense once an **experimental procedure** is specified—they are not “objective” apart from observer and apparatus; the procedure defines the observable, and disturbance yields spread.

The famous γ-ray microscope example was **criticized sharply by Bohr**—reportedly reducing Heisenberg to tears. Logically it sits awkwardly with “measurement = meaning”; quantitatively it fails because **repeated** position measurements (quantum Zeno) should agree, not differ after each “kick.”

Heisenberg kept revising. Under “measurement = meaning,” an **ontological** “objective” story is hard to sustain. More **operational** versions include:

- For a particle in a **known initial state**, predicted outcomes obey the uncertainty relation; or  
- Particles from a **fixed preparation procedure** (operational “same initial conditions”) show that spread.

That raises what “fixed preparation” means under quantum rules—perhaps only with Bohr’s **quantum/classical cut**, where preparation is classical. Such statements are **operational**, not verdicts on “objective determinacy.”

## 3. Definite “state”

Only if the **wave function uniquely fixes state** can we coherently define initial conditions and prove the modern textbook relation: for a **known initial quantum state**, outcomes obey the bound.

Early **matrix mechanics** clashed with Schrödinger’s **wave mechanics**: the wave function describes an unobserved, not-directly-observable state—Heisenberg attacked wave mechanics harshly in private; Schrödinger’s picture feels intuitive—a definite state at each instant, described by a **state vector**, not by position/momentum numbers.

Uncertainty often appears at **measurement (collapse)**—sometimes as **non-deterministic evolution** (probabilistic jump to an eigenstate), not “the state is fuzzy.” Generic states do not fix sharp measurement values; **eigenstates** do. Here the author prefers **“principle of imprecise measurement”** over “uncertainty”—what is fuzzy are **observables**, not necessarily the quantum state.

In an information view, unitary evolution conserves information; observation breaks that symmetry. If the wave function is physical, the uncertainty principle is substantive; if it is only epistemic, “fuzzy input → fuzzy output” is trivial.

Copenhagen readings often treat the wave function **epistemically**. Heisenberg favored names like “inexact relation” and “indeterminacy relation”; “uncertainty principle” spread later.
