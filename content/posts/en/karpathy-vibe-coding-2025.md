> Adapted from a Zhihu column: [Karpathy 2025 year in review](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/1986825153754206478). Summarized from public coverage; rights belong to the original author.

**Andrej Karpathy** (ex-Tesla AI, OpenAI founding team) published his 2025 LLM year in review. His headline take: AI is moving from “imitating humans” to “being its own thing”—weirder, but perhaps the **best time yet** to be a developer.

## 1. RLVR: reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards

The biggest training shift in 2025 is **RLVR**—models optimize against goals you can **automatically check** (tests pass, compiler happy, math answer correct).

- Less pure imitation of human demos; more trial-and-error on objectives
- Compute shifts from raw scale to **refining reasoning chains**
- Milestones: OpenAI o1/o3, DeepSeek R1, and similar lines

Karpathy frames this as “intelligence = useful trial and error,” not just bigger corpora.

## 2. Jagged intelligence

Capability profiles are **spiky**: expert-level in some tasks, silly mistakes in others. Leaderboard order matters less; **your** workflow matters more.

## 3. Vibe Coding: code as scratch paper

Karpathy’s throwaway term **Vibe Coding** went mainstream:

- Describe intent in plain language; AI drafts code
- Code is **disposable scratch**, not sacred asset
- Developers become spec owners and reviewers

PMs and ops started vibing small tools too. The new battleground: **context**—how agents remember prior vibes and project rules.

## 4. Application layer: Cursor and Claude Code

“LLM apps” are a new layer: context engineering, orchestration, autonomy sliders—not just chat UIs. **Cursor** and **Claude Code** are the poster children.

## 5. What it means for you

Upside: cheaper creativity, AI as external reasoning. Downside: tool churn—harnesses and prompts change behavior under your feet.

> We may still be using less than 10% of what today’s models can do. AI got weird in 2025—and that weirdness is what frees imagination in software.
