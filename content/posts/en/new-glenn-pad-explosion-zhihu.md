## What happened

**New Glenn**, the world’s second operational reusable heavy launcher in this framing, suffered a **ground static fire** for mission **NG-4** at **LC-36**, producing a fireball and mushroom cloud—the author suggests one of the largest **non-nuclear** mushroom clouds on U.S. soil this century.

- **When**: ~9 p.m. local time, May 28  
- **Hardware**: first stage with seven **BE-4** methalox engines  
- **Context**: pre-launch static test, not a full flight  
- **Damage**: booster likely destroyed; lightning tower down; pad and integration facilities heavily hit; repair may take months  
- **Crew**: no casualties reported so far  

Cloud scale is compared to **hundreds of tons of TNT equivalent**—harsher than many prior test failures.

## Likely causes

From early video, the author suspects **propellant leakage** and rapid methalox ignition.

The blast propagated **bottom-up** (unlike SpaceX’s Amos-6 pattern). Near BE-4, possibilities include overpressure, combustion instability, igniter faults, or seal failures mixing propellants.

After SpaceX’s V3 engine test success, **BE-4 credibility** may face more public doubt; combined with past upper-stage issues, schedule anxiety grows.

## Impacts on the Moon program and manifest

**Blue Moon** needs New Glenn for **Mark 1** cargo and **Mark 2 HLS**—NASA’s second Artemis lander provider alongside SpaceX.

- **LC-36** is New Glenn’s only pad; months of repair/investigation; maybe no flight until year-end  
- Plans for ~**12 launches** and **45 satellites** this year look unrealistic  
- Flight history noted: first flight with booster loss on recovery; second success; third success with payload issues; this stage was for a **fourth** attempt  

Mark 1 demos and Mark 2 development slip; a long probe could shake NASA confidence.

Early Artemis relies on **SLS** and Orion; HLS matters at landing; Starship adds redundancy. Blue Origin must recover fast to stay in the lunar roadmap.

## Why an orange-yellow flame?

Complete methalox burn is blue-white; imperfect combustion yields hot carbon particles and an **orange-yellow** plume—real engines rarely match textbook ideals.

## Closing

Liquid rockets have iterated through failure since Goddard. The author cites the booster name **“No, It’s Necessary”** from *Interstellar* and Musk’s line about learning through trial and error—**space is hard**.
