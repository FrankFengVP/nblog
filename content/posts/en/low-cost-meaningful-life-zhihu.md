## Core idea

**In today’s environment, the cost of survival is not high—but the cost of “face” is very high.** Drop face-saving and keeping up with others, and you can capture this era’s biggest dividend.

**A low-cost life may itself be the greatest dividend of our time.**

## The last decade: public goods at tiny personal cost

China built infrastructure at a scale and speed rarely seen elsewhere. Trillions in national capital may not land directly in every pocket, but ordinary people can enjoy public resources cheaply:

- **Travel**: high-speed rail, metro, expressways, city connectivity; cross-city trips in hours, cost per kilometer often better than any past era
- **Logistics**: phone cases for a few yuan with free shipping, T-shirts for pocket change—delivered in a day or two via a highly developed network
- **Daily life**: next-day and two-day delivery are normal

## “Meaningful life” infrastructure that is nearly free

| Area | Low-cost path |
|------|----------------|
| Reading & learning | Libraries on weekends: AC, seats, hot water; shelves as a gold mine of knowledge |
| Exercise | Urban greenways, park courts, community fitness corners; free park equipment and trails |
| Online learning | Bilibili: from Tsinghua circuits to Peking University philosophy, PS to programming—quality content for free |
| Healthcare | A dozen or twenty yuan for registration can mean face time with top clinicians—backed by public hospital subsidies and pooled resources |
| Formal education | National smart education platform and MOOCs: elite school recordings, academic lectures, free courses from primary school through grad prep |

## Dividend of the moment: AI and knowledge leveling

One of the most revolutionary dividends is **AI**:

- A child in a county town and a child in Beijing’s Haidian district can use the same AI, ask the same questions, and get answers of similar quality
- A decade ago, making a picture book meant writing, drawing, and layout; today you need more **imagination** and **the skill of asking good questions**

Knowledge is leveling further—the barrier shifts from “can you do X?” to “can you ask well?”

## Where does pressure come from? Face, comparison, consumerism

Some say: mortgages, raising kids, training classes—life is crushing.

The author is blunt: much of this is **personal choice**—comparison, or outcomes of choices you made yourself.

Industries often aim to empty your wallet. Consumerism’s slickest move is **tying products to identity**:

- Skip that face cream → you’re “not refined”
- Skip the lavish wedding → you “don’t love them”
- Skip the pricey summer camp → your child “loses at the starting line”

Seeing through that script is step one toward a low-cost, meaningful life.

## In a downturn: hold the line

When the economy slows, you can deliberately:

- Lower material wants, drop face games, keep cash
- Improve mental state and live more minimally

That is also a way to **steady your footing and recharge**—not giving up, but spending on what actually helps you grow.

## Takeaway

A meaningful life does not have to mean high spending. This era already offers:

1. Cheap public infrastructure and logistics  
2. Free entry points for reading, movement, and learning  
3. Subsidized healthcare and education platforms  
4. AI-driven knowledge leveling  

What’s truly expensive is often face and comparison. Put those down, and low cost can still be a very good life.
