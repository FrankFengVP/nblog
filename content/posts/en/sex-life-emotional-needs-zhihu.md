## Is sex life important?

**Yes—and also not entirely.**

The author’s core claim: people’s **primary need is emotional**, and sex is only one part—not the whole.

- Some bond first, then sex; others the reverse  
- For **most people**, emotion still comes first  
- Only with an emotional base does sex tend to feel harmonious  

Without movie-star looks, “instant physical pull” usually needs a relationship foundation; beauty is treated as the exception—ordinary couples are the focus below.

## Affairs: often misread as “about sex”

Many assume cheating is mainly sexual. The author argues it’s more often **unmet emotional value**.

- In Japan, some venues offer conversation only—no sex—yet many women still pay, seeking **being heard and affirmed**  
- Knowing someone is manipulative (“green tea” / “player”) yet staying: often because they’re **charming and emotionally rewarding**; even suspecting deception, people may accept tiny comfort  

After heartbreak, a skilled emotional provider can pull someone in despite risk—like lyrics about clinging to a sliver of sweetness.

## Do women have physical needs?

**Yes—but not always as much as stereotypes suggest.**

The author notes that in many cases, women’s sexuality **serves the relationship and male desire**—men often want more; women may use sex to please or as a “reward.” Those good with words often get physical closeness more easily.

Apparent flirtiness may be a **strategy for attention**, not nature; inside, someone may dislike it but do it to keep the bond.

Underneath, the need is often:

- To be touched, valued, praised  
- To avoid being treated as invisible or dismissed  

## Touch, words, and “paired companionship”

The author cites historical “paired companionship” among palace staff: without full intercourse, **hands and language** still convey love—sometimes purer than average marriages.

For him personally: **daily affection and sweet talk beat sex**—the former can happen every day; the latter cannot, and bodies have limits.

## The “thirties like wolves” myth

“Women in their thirties/forties are insatiable” is often misread.

In mid-life couples lacking:

- Coming home only to sleep  
- Rushing off without tender words  
- A wife dressing up but being ignored  

“She got horny” may mean **long without care**—using her body to get attention, wanting touch and regard—not necessarily more sex, but **to feel loved**.

Chronic lack of praise and affection withers confidence; then outside attention lands—and the rest writes itself.

## Advice to partners

- Don’t treat a partner as a “worn-out housewife” because of body changes; **praise and touch** matter  
- Contempt is reciprocal: you pull away, they pull away  
- “Kissing grosses me out for days” often means years without emotional play; steady interaction changes that  

The author jokes: for household peace, morning affection and praise help—even “without doing the full thing”—partners stay happier. That’s “life wisdom.”

## Sex vs love

- **Sex alone**: desire; can still feel empty after  
- **Sex with love**: fulfilling  

**Summary**: sex without love feels hollow; sex with love satisfies.

## Cycle timing

Side note: **ovulation** often raises desire (relative to other days). Timing can matter when discussing affairs.

Know your partner’s **cycle and rhythms**; neglect across years of marriage makes surprises less surprising—it signals inattention.

**Sex** matters, but not only sex; both need a sexual life, with **extra care around key cycle phases**; other days, touch and words can carry much of the load.
